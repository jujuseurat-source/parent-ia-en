Grabouilla had lost her rabbit.

That's how Romane started. No title, no draft. She wrote the witch's name at the top of the page and went.

Grabouilla is a witch she knows well (grabou bou, grabou bou, grabou bou, grabouilla). The rabbit's name was Cookie. I think Romane was a little hungry that evening, but she didn't confirm.

Cookie didn't exist. Romane has always wanted a rabbit. She doesn't have one. So Grabouilla had one, and Grabouilla had lost it.

## What AI does when it "creates"

When an AI writes a letter, a story, a poem, it doesn't start from nothing. It's read millions of texts: novels, letters, fairy tales, dialogues. And when you ask it to write something, it combines everything it's seen to produce something that looks like what you asked for.

That's not necessarily bad. But the process is different from what we imagine when we say "inventing."

The question nobody has really settled: is there a difference between "combining intelligently" and "creating"? Grabouilla and Cookie existed nowhere before Romane wrote their names. AI might have found a witch and a rabbit. But would it have found that rabbit, the one a child wanted and didn't have?

Maybe.

## The activity

Suggest to the child that they write a letter from an invented character to someone they've lost or who they miss. The character can be anything: a witch, a robot, a dragon, a knight. The person they're writing to as well.

To get things started, it helps to give two or three examples out loud, quickly, without lingering: a knight writing to his horse, a dragon writing to his cave, a fairy writing to her broken wand. Just to show that anything goes and the letter doesn't need to be long.

At the start of first grade, writing is new. A few words is enough. One or two sentences is already a letter. That's not a constraint, it's often the most honest thing.

For kids who are really just starting out or who freeze at a blank page: you can think it through together. The child gives the ideas (the character, who they're writing to, what they want to say), the adult writes it down. The letter still belongs to them. The words are theirs.

When the letter is done, ask the question: *"Do you think an AI could write a letter like yours?"* Let them answer before explaining anything.

## What actually happened

Romane wrote quickly, for herself. A few lines, approximate spelling, one word asked for along the way ("disappeared," she wanted to get it right). Grabouilla's letter was short: Cookie was gone, it was sad, she hoped he was eating well wherever he was.

She folded the page when I tried to read it.

When I asked about AI, she answered straight away: *"No. Because it doesn't know Grabouilla."*

I said AI had probably read thousands of children's songs and might have found Grabouilla somewhere.

*"Even the song?"*

I said it was possible.

She thought for a moment, then: *"But it never wanted a rabbit."*

I didn't have a very clear answer to that.

Meryl had been following from the sofa. When I tried to explain the activity to him, he asked if Cookie was a rabbit or a biscuit. I said a rabbit. He thought for a second. *"Both."* Then he went to get his cars.

## To finish

The letter is in Romane's room. She refused to show it to me because it was Grabouilla's letter, not mine.

Could an AI that has read every children's song, every witch story and every book about rabbits write that letter?

Maybe. But it never wanted Cookie.
