I read: *"Little Wolf doesn't want to..."*

Meryl answered: *"...sleep."*

Not because he'd analysed the sentence. Because the book is called **Little Wolf Doesn't Want to Sleep**, we were in the middle of the bedtime story, and that word was everywhere.

He said *"sleep"* several times.

Very seriously.

That's exactly what an AI does when it writes.

## What AI actually does when it speaks

When a tool like ChatGPT answers a question, it doesn't look up the right answer in a database. It doesn't think either, not in the way we mean.

It predicts the next word.

From everything it read during training, it calculates: which word is most likely to come after the words already written? It picks that word, does it again for the next one, and again. Until a full sentence forms.

Not because it knows what it wants to say.
Not because it understands the story.
Because it has seen an enormous number of sentences before.

It's statistical betting. Very sophisticated. Very fast.

And sometimes, like Meryl with *"sleep"*, it lands on the right word without really knowing why.

## The activity

Pick a book the child knows well. A bedtime picture book, a fairy tale, a story read a hundred times. Or, if you're lucky, a personalised book — one where their name actually appears in the text.

**First round: guess out loud.** Read a sentence and stop before the end. The child completes it. Then read the real continuation. Did they get it? Could their word have worked too?

**Second round: the next-word game.** The adult says one word. The child adds the one that feels natural after it. The adult goes again. You build a sentence together, one word each.

**Variation for laughs:** throw in words that don't fit. Words that derail the sentence. Kids usually prefer this part.

## What actually happened

Meryl didn't understand the game at first.

Which is fair.

At three and a half, the bedtime story isn't a laboratory. It's the story. The moment. The pictures. The pleasure of finding Little Wolf again.

He was completely in it.

I read. I stopped. He looked at the picture. He said *"sleep."*

Then at one point I left a sentence open at the end.

He answered:

*"A little... CUDDLE."*

I told him no, it was actually *"wee"*.

He hadn't got it.

But straight after, he looked at the book. And showed me the picture.

Little Wolf was doing a wee.

He hadn't guessed the word. But he'd seen that the picture was telling him something. He wasn't just using words — he was using context, everything he had in front of him.

That's actually what the best AI systems are starting to do too.

Romane had a different book. Her personalised one: **Romane, Father Christmas Needs You**, a gift from her uncle. It's long enough that she doesn't know it by heart. She had to genuinely guess.

When I stopped:

*"In the sky..."*

She suggested:

*"starry."*

It wasn't the word in the book.

I told her:

*"That could work too."*

She said:

*"AI can find the words."*

Very accurate. Not complete, not technical. Very accurate.

We kept going. Building sentences together, one word each. Instinctive for her.

I asked:

*"But how do you find your words?"*

*"They just come into my head."*

There it is.

That's probably one of the best explanations possible. Words come because she's heard sentences, stories, beginnings, endings. Because she feels what can follow.

An AI doesn't feel.

But it does something that looks like that, with far more examples.

Then we tried with strange words. Things went off the rails. Which was much better.

Romane wanted to keep going. Sentences that half held together. Words landing in the wrong place.

Later, with her brother, they kept going at the dinner table while I was cooking.

I wasn't running anything anymore.

They were playing with word sequences.

And honestly, sometimes an AI would have struggled to keep up.

## To finish

We didn't have a big lesson on probability.

We read **Little Wolf Doesn't Want to Sleep**.

Meryl said *"sleep"* like the whole story lived in that one word. He tried *"cuddle"* instead of *"wee"*, then showed me the picture to prove he'd still understood something.

Romane suggested *"starry"* instead of the book's word. And she was right — it could have worked.

Then she told me AI can find the words. That in her head, words just come on their own.

I don't know if AI would say the same.

It would probably predict: *"Words come..."*

And choose *"naturally."*

Which isn't wrong.
